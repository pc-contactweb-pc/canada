Frontcountry camping

An intermediate option

Frontcountry refers to things beside, or near, a road. When it comes to camping, this is often described as "car camping" as they are easily accessible by cars or RVs What are: RVs

A great option for beginners. Frontcountry campgrounds usually include:

  • washrooms with showers
  • kitchen shelters
  • electrical
  • sewer and water hookups
  • Wi-Fi access zones

Campgrounds may also be close to outdoor theatres, beaches, grocery stores, playgrounds and picnic shelters. Sites may have a fire pit, or access to a shared fire pit.

Frontcountry campgrounds are generally easy to access by car, and RVs are welcome in many locations.

A few locations also offer equipped camping: we provide the camping equipment you’ll need (tent, stove, lantern, etc.), thanks to our partnership with MEC.

What are:RVs

Recreational Vehicles, also called RVs, "campers", "campervans" or "caravans" are sleeping areas on wheels. They usually offer more convenience and security compared to tents.

Some RVs have built in heaters, kitchens, toilets, or even showers.

The term RVs is used as a catch all term for any vehicle with a sleeping area built in.

RVs are further grouped based on their features, how they are built, or

When speaking about RVs specifically, people will use the term RVs to describe driveable RVs which you pull behind a car as a "Camper"

1. Vehicles you drive ("RVs")

Motorhomes

Larger motorhomes with a built in "coach" style cab, similar to a bus.

Campervans

Some are smaller motorhomes, usually with a defined cab

Others are more traditional van size. Some may include pop-up sections

2. Vehicles you tow ("Campers", or "Camp trailers")

Travel trailers

Also a catch-all phrase for all trailers, travel trailers usually refer to lighter built trailers with a regular hitch.

Fifth wheel campers

Usually the largest of campers or camp trailers, fifth wheel trailers require a special hitch. They are identifiable by the unique front.

Pop-ups

Usually the smallest and lightest of camp trailers, these trailers fold flatter while moving, before "popping up" to provide more room when camping.

Backcountry camping

A little more difficult

Looking for more of a challenge? Try the ultimate outdoor adventure: you, your camping gear and the great outdoors.

Your backcountry campsite might include:

  • a tent pad
  • picnic tables
  • outhouses
  • firewood
  • a fire pit

Most backcountry sites are not accessible by car, and you can expect to do some hiking, canoeing, or kayaking to get to them.
